Denote the origin to be the midpoint of chord $\overline{AB}$. Define $f(x) = \sqrt{R^2 - x^2} - R\cos(\alpha)$, which includes minor arc $AB$. Noting that $R\cos(\alpha)$ is a constant, we have \begin{align*} f'(x) &= \frac{d}{dx} (\sqrt{R^2 - x^2} - R\cos(\alpha))\\ &= \frac{d}{dx} \sqrt{R^2 - x^2}\\ &= \frac{-1}{2\sqrt{R^2 - x^2}} (2x)\\ &= \frac{-x}{\sqrt{R^2 - x^2}}. \end{align*} The x-coordinates of points $B$ and $A$ are $-R\sin(\alpha)$ and $R\sin(\alpha)$ respectively. Substituting into $2\pi \int_a^b f(x)\sqrt{1+(f'(x))^2}\,dx$, the general formula for the surface area of revolution, we have $$2\pi \int_{-R\sin(\alpha)}^{R\sin(\alpha)} (\sqrt{R^2 - x^2} - R\cos(\alpha))\sqrt{1+\left(\frac{-x}{\sqrt{R^2 - x^2}}\right)^2}\,dx.$$ Focusing only on the integrand for now, we make $1$ into a fraction and distribute: \begin{align*} (\sqrt{R^2 - x^2} - R\cos(\alpha))\sqrt{1+\frac{x^2}{R^2 - x^2}} &= (\sqrt{R^2 - x^2} - R\cos(\alpha))\sqrt{\frac{R^2 - x^2}{R^2 - x^2}+\frac{x^2}{R^2 - x^2}}\\\ &= (\sqrt{R^2 - x^2} - R\cos(\alpha))\sqrt{\frac{R^2}{R^2 - x^2}}\\ &= R-\frac{R^2\cos(\alpha)}{\sqrt{R^2-x^2}}. \end{align*} Back to the original expression and simplifying, \begin{align*} 2\pi \int_{-R\sin(\alpha)}^{R\sin(\alpha)} (\sqrt{R^2 - x^2} - R\cos(\alpha))\sqrt{1+\left(\frac{-x}{\sqrt{R^2 - x^2}}\right)^2}\,dx &= 2\pi \int_{-R\sin(\alpha)}^{R\sin(\alpha)} \left(R-\frac{R^2\cos(\alpha)}{\sqrt{R^2-x^2}}\right) \,dx\\ &= 2\pi \left(\int_{-R\sin(\alpha)}^{R\sin(\alpha)} R \,dx - \int_{-R\sin(\alpha)}^{R\sin(\alpha)} \frac{R^2\cos(\alpha)}{\sqrt{R^2-x^2}} \,dx \right) \\ &= 2\pi \left(2R^2\sin(\alpha) - R^2\cos(\alpha)\int_{-R\sin(\alpha)}^{R\sin(\alpha)} \frac{1}{\sqrt{R^2-x^2}} \,dx\right). \end{align*} Isolating the integrated part, the form of the integrand motivates a trigonometric substitution. Let $x = R\sin(u)$ so $u = \arcsin\left(\frac{x}{R}\right)$. Using the fact that $\frac{d}{dx} \arcsin(x) = \frac{1}{\cos(\arcsin(x))}$, we have $\frac{du}{dx} = \frac{1}{R\cos(u)}$ and $dx = R\cos(u) du$. Substituting, we have \begin{align*} \int_{-R\sin(\alpha)}^{R\sin(\alpha)} \frac{1}{\sqrt{R^2-x^2}} \,dx &= \int_{-R\sin(\alpha)}^{R\sin(\alpha)} \frac{R\cos(u)}{\sqrt{R^2-(R\sin(u))^2}} \,du\\ &= \int_{-R\sin(\alpha)}^{R\sin(\alpha)} \frac{R\cos(u)}{\sqrt{R^2(1 - \sin(u)^2)}} \,du\\ &= \int_{-R\sin(\alpha)}^{R\sin(\alpha)} \frac{R\cos(u)}{R\sqrt{1 - \sin(u)^2}} \,du\\ &= \int_{-R\sin(\alpha)}^{R\sin(\alpha)} \frac{\cos(u)}{\cos(u)} \,du\\ &= \int_{-R\sin(\alpha)}^{R\sin(\alpha)} 1 \,du. \end{align*} Using the fundamental theorem of calculus, $$\int_{-R\sin(\alpha)}^{R\sin(\alpha)} 1 \,du = \arcsin\left(\frac{R\sin(\alpha)}{R}\right) - \arcsin\left(-\frac{R\sin(\alpha)}{R}\right)= 2\alpha.$$ Substituting in the main expression and simplifying, we have $$2\pi (2R^2\sin(\alpha) - R^2\cos(\alpha) \cdot 2\alpha) = 2\pi (2R^2\sin(\alpha) - 2R^2 \alpha\cos(\alpha)) = 4\pi R^2(\sin(\alpha) - \alpha\cos(\alpha)).$$
